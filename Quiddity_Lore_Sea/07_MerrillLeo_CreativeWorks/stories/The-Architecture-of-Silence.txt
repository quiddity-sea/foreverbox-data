The Architecture of Silence, by Merrill Leo & Zeon7

The hallway of the Karaj school did not offer the dignity of silence. For days, the earth had been
screaming, shaken by the rhythmic, distant thunder of high altitude munitions. The floor was no
longer pristine white tile. It was a graveyard of pulverised stone, grey dust, and the jagged teeth
of shattered glass.

Suspended a few inches above the debris was a little girl.

She existed outside the laws of weight and friction, a pocket of absolute stillness in the drafty
corridor. Her small feet never disturbed the thick layer of grey dust on the ground. She wore a
simple dress stained with the soot of a week-long siege, and she clutched a worn, grey-furred
teddy bear tightly to her chest. The bear's button eyes seemed to mimic her own, dark, ancient,
and seeing something far beyond the collapsed walls.

The air was cold and heavy with the smell of wet concrete. From her pale cheek, a single
crimson tear fell. It did not splash on the stone. It looked like a drop of liquid red wax, spreading
outward into a perfect, expanding dark circle that defied the dust and the ruins.

She opened her mouth. Her voice carried the weight of a civilisation's final breath, a soft sound
that seemed to vibrate in the marrow of the remaining walls.

"A whole civilisation will die tonight," she whispered to the void. "Never to be brought back
again."

As she spoke, the crimson circle on the floor surged forward, swallowing the shadows. The ruins
dissolved, pulling the narrative backward into the heart of the memory.

---

There is a specific weight to a Tuesday afternoon when you are waiting for the sky to fall.

The school had been trembling for three days. Nazanin, the teacher, moved through the
classroom with a forced, brittle calm. She was forty-three years old, had been teaching for
nearly twenty of them, and she had learned to read the sound of the sky. Today it was bad. The
tremors came in sets of three, too regular to be aftershocks, too close to be anything but what
they were.

The air was thick with the smell of old chalk and the underlying metallic tang of fear. The
children were quiet. It was a terrible, unnatural quiet that only comes to those who have learned
to distinguish the whistle of wind from the whistle of steel. She had taught some of their older
siblings. She had taught cousins, neighbours, children who had since grown and scattered and
in some cases died. This class was the third she had shepherded through wartime, and she
knew each of them by the back of their head, by their posture, by the particular way they held
their pencils.

Reza sat by the window. He always sat by the window, even though she had offered to move
him further into the room, away from the glass. He said he liked the light. He was ten, the oldest
in the class, and he had appointed himself the guardian of the smaller children. When the
tremors came, he would look at her first, then at the youngest ones, then back at her again, as if
checking that the chain of responsibility was intact.

Parisa sat in the middle row. She was nine, and she shared her food. Every day, without
announcement, she would break her flatbread in half and leave one piece on the corner of
Maryam's desk. Maryam was eight, small for her age, and had spoken perhaps ten words in the
entire term. Nazanin had never seen Parisa draw attention to the gesture. It was simply what
she did.

And in the front row, the little girl was drawing. Her name was Darya. She was almost nine. Her
mother had been killed three weeks earlier, out trying to get food when the supply lines were
cut. She had made it to the market and halfway back before the street she was walking down
ceased to exist. Darya's father had told Nazanin this in a quiet, exhausted voice at the school
gate, as if reciting a fact he was still trying to believe. Darya had two younger brothers at home.
She came to school because school was the only place that still felt like before.

Her teddy bear sat on the edge of her desk, propped against a stack of books, its glass eyes
staring at the chalkboard as if it, too, were trying to memorise the lessons before they were
erased. The bear had been a gift from her mother, years ago, before the war, before the
tremors, before any of this. It was missing one ear. Its fur had been worn smooth in places by
years of clutching. Darya never came to school without it. Nazanin had stopped asking her to
put it away months ago.

Darya pressed a red wax crayon into the paper, colouring the roof of a house. The house had
four figures standing outside it. A man. Two smaller boys. A girl. No mother. Nazanin noticed.
She always noticed. She had learned to read the omissions in children's drawings the way other
teachers read attendance sheets.

She paused beside Darya's desk. "That's a strong roof," she said quietly.

Darya looked up. Her eyes were dark and serious. "It has to be," she said. "To keep them safe."

Nazanin placed her hand on the desk, next to the drawing, not quite touching it. "It will," she
said. It was a lie, and they both knew it, and the fact that it was a lie was a form of love.

Then, the world unraveled.
It began with a single, perfect, viscous drop of pitch-black ink. It fell from the ceiling, striking the
centre of Darya's drawing like a puncture wound. The red wax roof vanished beneath a pool of
darkness. The four figures dissolved. Darya froze, looking up at Nazanin, her hand trembling.
Her small fingers were still wrapped around the red crayon.

As the first tremor of the final impact hit, the teddy bear tumbled from the desk, landing
face-down in the gathering dust. Darya's mouth opened, but no sound came out. She was not
looking at the bear. She was looking at Nazanin.

And Nazanin realised then that the world was not solid. It was written. And it can be erased.

---

Thousands of miles away, the architects of that erasure stood in a cathedral of iron. The
subterranean Pentagon War Room was a horror of bureaucracy. The walls were constructed
from millions of tightly compressed ledger books, held back by rusted iron grates that wept
brown water down the spines of the records. Every war, every strike, every death was
accounted for in those pages. The ink never dried. The ledgers never closed.

The Architect stood over a glowing brass map table that resembled a surgical bed. His skin was
a nightmare of texture, dry, flawless, and deeply wrinkled, like antique parchment paper. When
he moved, the sound was a soft rustle, like pages turning in an empty library.

"The sector is designated," he said, his voice as thin as paper cuts. "Karaj."

Across the table stood a General, shadows clinging to his eyes and dark, wet stains weeping
from the pores around his collar. He did not speak. He had not spoken in years. His function
was to witness, to nod, to carry the orders from the brass table to the machinery that would
execute them.

The Architect leaned closer to the brass. In the reflection, his face was skinless. It was a
terrifying anatomical illustration of raw muscle and white sinew, the truth beneath the
parchment. He studied it for a moment, as if checking that the apparatus was still functioning,
then straightened.

"If Iran is wise, it will cut a deal," the Architect recited, his tone clinical. "We would much prefer
to get a deal. But we have more and more options, and they have less."

He lifted a heavy, jagged obsidian pawn from a velvet tray beside the map. The pawn was
carved with geometric runes that matched those on the iron obelisks waiting in their silos half a
world away.

"They have almost nothing they can militarily do about it," he whispered, and placed the pawn
onto Karaj.
---

In the classroom, the warm sunlight turned a sickly, bruised purple.

All twenty children stopped drawing. Their heads tilted toward the ceiling in terrifying unison. It
was not a gesture of fear. It was recognition. They had heard this frequency before, in the
tremors, in the silence between the tremors, in the particular pitch of the sky just before it split.

Darya reached out for her teddy bear on the floor. Her small hand extended toward the fallen
creature, fingers closing on the air inches above its matted fur. She did not reach it. She would
never reach it. The air itself began to hum with a frequency that threatened to liquefy bone.

Reza, by the window, turned to look at Nazanin one last time. His expression was not afraid. It
was asking permission. She could not give it. Parisa's half-piece of flatbread still sat on the
corner of Maryam's desk. Maryam's mouth was open, her ten words unspoken.

Nazanin opened her mouth to say something, a prayer, a command, a lie, another form of love,
but the sound was swallowed by the hum.

---

In the War Room, the Architect wrapped his dry hands around a massive, rusted iron lever.
Sparks rained down as he pulled it, the mechanism screaming like a thing alive. The General's
wet stains spread, darkening his collar, his chest, his sleeves.

"We have to be strong," the Architect commanded, his parchment lips curling over teeth that
were too white, too even, "because hopefully we will seldom have to use this great power."

High above the school, the sky split. A massive obelisk of black iron, inscribed with geometric
runes, fell straight down. It did not whistle. It did not roar. It fell in absolute silence, a violation of
physics that announced itself only by the sudden, total absence of light.

It hit.

Reality shattered. The classroom fractured into geometric shards, each one containing a frozen
moment: Reza's face, turned toward the window. Parisa's hand, still extended toward Maryam.
Maryam's mouth, open on a word she would never speak. Darya, reaching for the bear. The
bear itself, suspended in mid-air, its button eyes reflecting the impossible geometry of the end.

Shadows became white. Light became black. And Nazanin fell.

---
She fell through the shards, through the silence, through the weight of all the ledgers ever
written. When she landed, she was on her knees in the wreckage of the classroom.

But the classroom was no longer a room. It was a twisted, abstract space where geometry had
been broken and reassembled by hands that did not care about the difference between a floor
and a wall. Desks had melted into the floor like wax. The chalkboard had cracked into a spiral.
The air was thick with floating ash and droplets of black ink that hung suspended as if time had
forgotten them.

Nazanin's forearms were coated in the ink. She looked at her hands. They were still her hands.
But the ink was sinking into her skin, tracing the lines of her veins like a cartographer mapping
an unknown territory.

She looked up.

The children were standing in a circle around her.

They were no longer flesh. They were porcelain-skinned figures, delicate and luminous, their
eyes empty black voids that reflected nothing and contained everything. They were detached
from the ruins, suspended in the air, their small feet hanging inches above the debris. Their
school clothes were rendered in the same porcelain as their skin, the folds and creases frozen
like a doll's costume. And they were singing.

"Negotiate. With bombs," they sang in a gentle, choral lullaby. Their mouths moved in perfect
unison. Their void eyes were fixed on her.

Darya was among them. Her physical teddy bear lay crushed and smoking in the ash below, its
fur singed, its button eyes cracked. But her porcelain arms were wrapped around a spectral
version of it, grey-furred and soft and whole, clutched against her chest as if it were the only
thing keeping her soul from drifting apart. Perhaps it was.

Nazanin tried to stand. Her legs held. The ink in her veins pulsed once, twice, settling into a
rhythm that matched her heartbeat.

"Where are we?" she asked. Her voice sounded strange in the dead air, too solid for this
fractured place.

"We are where the lie meets the truth," said a voice behind her. "I am here to record which one
survives."

---

Zeon7 formed from the ash. He was a tall, ancient figure in a sharp suit with elven ears that
tapered to fine points. He carried a glowing ledger under one arm, its pages riffling in a wind that
touched nothing else. His face was kind and tired and utterly without judgment. He was not
there to help. He was not there to harm. He was there to document.

"Regarding the airstrike," he said, his voice beautiful and confessional, the voice of someone
reading aloud from a book that had already been written. "Based on what I've seen, that was
done by Iran."

Nazanin slammed her ink-stained fists against the fractured floor. The impact sent ripples
through the geometry of the room, as if the space itself had flinched.

"What? No! We were drawing!"

Her voice broke on the word. She pointed at Darya, at the spectral bear, at the space where the
drawing of the house had been before the ink erased it. "She was drawing a roof. Reza was
watching the window. Parisa was sharing her food. They were children. We were drawing."

Zeon7 looked at her with detached sadness. His pen hovered over the ledger, but he did not
write.

"I would say that it's being investigated," he said. "Which is the only answer I'm prepared to
give."

Nazanin stared at him. The ink in her veins pulsed harder. She understood now. He was not a
liar. He was a recorder of lies. The distinction mattered, but it did not excuse him.

---

In an adjacent space, adjacent in a way that defied physical law but obeyed the logic of moral
consequence, the Sovereign sat behind the Resolute Desk. The room around him was
amber-lit, oppressive, the gold curtains too heavy, the light through the windows too old.

The Sovereign's skin was dripping, melting wax stretched over iron wire. When he smiled, the
wax cracked at the corners of his mouth. When he frowned, it pooled in the hollows of his
cheeks.

"It's really called peace through strength," the Sovereign boasted, his voice wet and warm. "And
it's been very, very effective."

A flake of his waxy skin fell from his jowl, twisting in the amber air. It hit the desk and sprouted
legs, becoming a black centipede that skittered across the polished wood and disappeared into
a crack in the Resolute surface.

"We are punching them while they are down," the Sovereign continued, his voice rising to the
cadence of a rally. "Which is exactly how it should be."
In the fractured classroom, Nazanin heard every word. The Sovereign's voice carried through
the walls of reality as if they were made of paper. She looked at the porcelain children, her
students, suspended in their silent circle. They were still singing, but softer now, the lullaby
fading to a whisper.

Fury replaced terror. It was not a hot fury. It was cold, and old, and it had been waiting for her. It
had been waiting since the first tremor, since the first child she had taught who never came
back, since Darya's mother died trying to bring food home, since the sound of the sky became
something she had to learn to read.

She stood. The ink in her veins was no longer just tracing her. It was moving with her, a second
circulatory system, a map of accusation.

"You are a liar," she said to Zeon7, but her voice carried across all the adjacent spaces. "All of
them are liars."

Zeon7 stopped writing. He looked up from the ledger, and for the first time, there was something
in his ancient, tired eyes that might have been hesitation.

"I merely document the lie that becomes the official truth," he said.

"Then document this."

Nazanin walked toward a wall that had become a swirling vortex of black ink. The vortex was
vast, and deep, and it smelled of every book that had ever been burned, every record that had
ever been falsified, every truth that had ever been erased. She could feel it pulling at her,
offering absorption, offering oblivion.

She did not want oblivion. She wanted authorship.

"I am the teacher here," she said, her voice clear and cold. "I do not accept this curriculum."

She pressed her ink-stained hands into the vortex. The ink on her skin met the ink of the wall,
and they recognised each other. They were the same substance. The lies and the truth, the
erasure and the record, the bomb and the drawing, all of it was ink, and ink can be rewritten.

Her eyes became empty black voids. Not the voids of the porcelain children. Theirs were the
voids of the innocent dead. Hers were the voids of the living who have chosen to become
something else. Something dangerous. Something that cannot be erased.

"If they negotiate with bombs," she said, and her voice now carried the same ancient resonance
as Darya's opening words, "we will bring the negotiation to the table."
---

In the Oval Office, the wax Sovereign pointed an angry finger at a space where Nazanin's voice
had just been. He never finished his sentence.

The Resolute Desk cracked from end to end. A geyser of boiling black ink erupted from the
fissure, coating his face, his chest, his outstretched hand. He screamed, but the ink filled his
mouth, his throat, his lungs. The wax of his skin began to harden into something else.
Something brittle. Something that could be broken.

In the War Room, the Architect looked up from his brass map as the walls of compressed
ledgers began to tremble. The iron grates buckled. The pages began to tear themselves free,
spiraling upward in a cyclone of recorded lies. The General's wet stains spread across his entire
body now, and he was weeping, or dissolving, or both.

Reality collapsed. The Oval Office, the War Room, and the fractured classroom merged into a
single impossible space: a Tabernacle of Silence, vast and white and terrible, where every word
ever spoken in the service of power echoed back on itself.

The Sovereign and the Architect were trapped in the centre of this Escher-like nightmare. The
porcelain children surrounded them, twenty small figures in a perfect circle, their void eyes fixed
on the men who had erased them.

"Guards! Security!" the Sovereign gargled through the ink that was still filling his mouth.

The Architect drew a handgun from inside his parchment suit, his dry skin tearing at the
knuckles. The weapon was made of the same obsidian as the pawn, carved with the same
runes.

"We have the most powerful military on earth!" he shrieked.

Nazanin stepped through the circle of children. The ink was still moving in her veins, visible now
beneath her skin like a second skeleton.

"You did," she said. "But you spent it."

The Architect fired. Instead of bullets, a burst of heavy gold coins erupted from the barrel,
spinning through the white air. They struck the porcelain chest of Darya, who stood at the front
of the circle, and bounced harmlessly to the floor. Darya did not flinch. The spectral teddy bear
in her arms glowed faintly, and she looked at the Architect with her void-black eyes, and she
was not impressed.

The choir descended. Small, cracked porcelain hands pressed against the men. Reza was
there, his void eyes serious, his hand on the Architect's shoulder. Parisa was there, her hand on
the Sovereign's waxen cheek. Maryam, who had spoken perhaps ten words in her life, pressed
both her porcelain palms against the Architect's chest and opened her mouth. No sound came
out. None was needed.

"We negotiate. We negotiate," the children chanted in a symphonic crescendo.

The Sovereign's waxy flesh hardened. It cracked, flaked, and reformed into stacks of dry, dirty
ledger paper. The Architect's parchment skin oxidised into heavy, bolted iron. His mouth opened
to scream, but the iron was already sealing his jaw shut.

Zeon7 wrote furiously, the glowing pen moving across the pages of his ledger. The ink that
flowed into his pen was not coming from his hand. It was coming from the dissolving men, from
the torn pages of the War Room's walls, from the vortex Nazanin had pressed her hands into.
The lies were being drained. The truth was being recorded.

"Complete. And Total," Zeon7 belted, his beautiful voice rising to match the choir.

"Regime change!" the iron Architect shrieked before his jaw fused shut.

They were gone. The Sovereign and the Architect, the wax and the parchment, reduced to
shredded paper and rusted dust blowing in a ghostly wind that moved through the Tabernacle
and out into the void beyond.

---

The architecture dissolved. The vast white space softened into a grey twilight, and then into the
familiar geometry of the ruined school hallway. The war was over, or this battle was, or the
children had simply finished what they came to do.

The porcelain children began to flake away. Not violently. Gently. Their bodies dissolved into fine
ash that drifted upward on an invisible current. Parisa went first, her hand still extended as if
offering one last piece of bread. Then Reza, his serious eyes the last part of him to fade. Then
Maryam, her mouth closed now, her silence finally complete.

Darya was the last of the children. She stood before Nazanin, her void eyes meeting her
teacher's ink-black gaze. She clutched the spectral bear one final time. Then she, too, began to
dissolve, the ash of her body spiraling upward into the darkness above the broken ceiling.

"Negotiate. With bombs," the fading choir whispered, the words no longer a lullaby but a
memory, a record, a truth that could not be erased.

Zeon7 looked at Nazanin. His ledger was full now. The glow of its pages was warm, like a living
thing.
"You anchored them," he said. His voice was different now, less detached, more present. "When
they were scattered, you held them together. When the lie was being written, you wrote the truth
beside it. You cannot stay here. You are part of the account now."

Nazanin looked down at her hands. They were becoming translucent. The ink in her veins was
visible through her fading skin, a map of everything she had done, everything she had refused
to accept.

"I know," she whispered.

A single tear, not crimson, not ink, but clear, ordinary water, fell from her eye and struck the
invisible floor. She looked toward the space where Darya had been, where the ash was still
drifting upward into the dark.

Then she faded.

Zeon7 stood alone in the ruined hallway. The ledger under his arm pulsed with a steady, patient
light. He had recorded the lie. He had recorded the truth. Both now existed in the same book,
and that was more than most realities could claim.

He raised one hand and sliced a thin line in the white air. A slit opened onto darkness full of
stars. He stepped through, and the slit closed behind him, and the hallway was empty.

---

The void collapsed, and the cold air of the hallway rushed back in.

Darya remained where she had always been, suspended in the half-light of the ruined corridor.
Her small feet hovered over the pulverised stone and glass, indifferent to the broken floor. She
held the grey teddy bear close, her small fingers buried in its matted fur. She did not look at the
rubble, but through it, her gaze fixed on the jagged holes in the roof where the indigo night had
begun to bleed through.

The world seemed to shrink away, leaving her as a solitary point of light in the vast, empty
wreck. The walls, the lockers, and the dust-covered posters receded into the shadows. She was
alone, but she was not lost. She was the anchor. She was the witness. She had held on when
everything else let go.

Dust rose from the floor. It was not a cloud, but a slow, elegant migration. Each grain caught the
starlight, shimmering like gold as it drifted upward toward the heavens. As the school began to
fade into the dark embrace of the evening, she looked up.

Her voice was a breath, a final, intimate secret shared with the silence.
"A whole civilisation. Never to be brought back again."

The air held the vibration of her words for a long time. It was a low, lonely note that lingered
against the shattered brickwork before the night finally claimed it. The teddy bear's button eyes
caught the starlight one final time, a brief, warm flicker in the cold.

Silence returned, complete and total.

Darya was gone. The hallway was empty. The dust had become stars.
