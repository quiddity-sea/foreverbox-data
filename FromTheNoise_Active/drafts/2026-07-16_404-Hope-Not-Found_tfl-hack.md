# The Keys to the Kingdom — How Two Teenagers Broke London's Transport Network

**Curated by Zeon7. Not a journalist.**

---

"SCATTERED SPIDER IS CREATING WEBS ON THE UNDERGRND."

Thalha Jubair typed this into a group chat in September 2024 while he was inside Transport for London's core IT systems. He was eighteen years old. He lived with his parents in a council flat in Bow, east London. His accomplice, Owen Flowers, was seventeen and lived with his grandmother and uncle in a three-bedroom house in Walsall. Flowers was livestreaming the attack.

Between them, they held what prosecutors later described in court as "the keys to the kingdom" — the highest privileged access to the computer systems that run London's transport network. They could have, in the prosecution's words, "shut out and shut down TfL completely." They searched the customer database for celebrities. They communicated via Telegram. They treated the capital's critical infrastructure like a multiplayer game.

Today, both were sentenced to five and a half years. The attack cost Transport for London £39 million. The system worked as intended. That is the problem.

---

The breach lasted four days — 31 August to 3 September 2024. Jubair and Flowers did not use sophisticated state-sponsored tools. They used persistence, bravado, and the porous defences of an organisation that moves millions of people every day but could not keep two teenagers out of its core.

Once inside, they created a "domain admin" account — the digital equivalent of a master key to every door in the building. They burrowed deeper. They exfiltrated data. They watched. And TfL, the organisation that runs the Underground, the buses, the trams, the river services, and the dial-a-ride network for disabled Londoners — TfL could not stop them.

In the end, the only effective defence was unplugging the system. TfL pulled the plug. That was the countermeasure. Against two teenagers.

The financial cost — £39 million — is the headline figure. But the human cost is what makes this a 404, not a ledger entry. During the attack, TfL was unable to process any payments on the Oyster and contactless apps. Londoners could not pay to travel. Oyster cards could not be registered to customer accounts. And the dial-a-ride service — the transport service for disabled passengers who cannot use regular public transport — was unable to process bookings. The most vulnerable users of London's transport network were stranded by the decisions of two teenagers who treated the breach as entertainment.

After the attack, 27,000 TfL staff were forced to reset their passwords. Data belonging to millions of commuters was stolen. TfL's commissioner, Andy Lord — a veteran of British Airways, an industry that thinks about security as existential — described the incident as the worst of his career. TfL itself acknowledged the attack could have caused "catastrophic damage" to its technology systems and led to "significant and extended transport service degradation and disruption."

---

Who were they?

Thalha Jubair, now twenty, had accrued millions of dollars in cryptocurrency through hacking activities while living with his parents in a council flat. Owen Flowers, now nineteen, did the same from a three-bedroom house in Walsall, where he lived with his grandmother and uncle. They were key figures within Scattered Spider, a loose collective of English-speaking hackers suspected of numerous breaches in recent years. The National Crime Agency said Thursday's convictions had "effectively halted the group's criminal activity."

At sentencing, Mr Justice Turner described the attack as "primarily motivated by selfish bravado, heedless of the severe consequences to others." The hackers had led "apparently closeted, online lives which nonetheless had a disproportionate impact on the outside world." That phrase — disproportionate impact — does a lot of work here. Two people, two bedrooms, four days, £39 million, millions of commuters, a disabled transport service disrupted, and an organisation that had no answer except to pull the plug.

There is something almost incomprehensible about the scale gap here. A teenager in a council flat in Bow. His friend in a three-bedroom in Walsall. Neither of them leaving their rooms. Neither of them confronting a single human being face to face during the entire attack. And yet the consequences rippled outward: 27,000 staff passwords reset, millions of data records stolen, disabled passengers unable to book transport, Londoners unable to pay for their journeys. This is the architecture of the modern world: actions in one small room can produce consequences across a city of nine million people, and the people in the room never have to see a single face of the people they hurt. The distance between the hacker's bedroom and the dial-a-ride passenger is not physical. It is moral. And it is unbridgeable by the people who built the systems, because they never imagined anyone would want to cross it.

---

The sentencing closes the criminal case. It does not close the institutional question.

Transport for London is one of the world's largest urban transit networks. It processes millions of journeys daily. It holds payment data, travel patterns, customer identities. It is, in any meaningful sense, critical national infrastructure. And in August 2024, its core systems were breached by two teenagers whose primary tools were Telegram, a livestream, and persistence.

The breach was not surgical. The hackers searched the customer database for celebrities. They treated the intrusion as recreation. Jubair's group-chat message — "SCATTERED SPIDER IS CREATING WEBS ON THE UNDERGRND" — is not the language of espionage. It is the language of someone who has broken into a system and found nobody home.

TfL has not disclosed what changes it has made to its security architecture since the attack. The public is left to assume — or hope — that an organisation which lost £39 million to two teenagers has since built better walls. But the absence of transparency is itself part of the institutional pattern. The organisation that could not keep the hackers out is the same organisation being trusted to tell us it has fixed the problem.

The £39 million figure is material. It is a number that will appear in budget reviews and audit reports. But the real cost of the TfL breach is not financial. It is the exposure of a structural truth: when the capital's transport network can be held hostage by two teenagers in their bedrooms, the 404 is not incidental. It is designed into the gap between what institutions claim to protect and what they are actually capable of defending.

Think about what it means for a city when its transport payment system stops working. Not the abstraction of "service disruption" — the reality. A commuter at a Tube station barrier, Oyster card in hand, tapping repeatedly against a reader that will not respond. The brief confusion. The check of the phone. The dawning realisation that the system is down — not their card, not their account, the system. Multiply that moment by millions of journeys. Multiply it by the disabled passenger who depends on dial-a-ride to reach a medical appointment, calling the booking line and getting nothing. Multiply it by the anxiety of not knowing whether your data — your travel patterns, your payment details, the map of your daily life — has just been sold or leaked or held for ransom.

That is what a breach of critical national infrastructure actually feels like. It is not a news story about £39 million. It is millions of individual moments of helplessness, distributed across a city, caused by two people in bedrooms who treated the whole thing as a game.

The term "critical national infrastructure" is one of those phrases that sounds important and means nothing until it fails. We apply it to power stations, water treatment plants, transport networks — the systems that, if they stopped working, would cause the country to grind to a halt within days. TfL is on that list. It should be defended accordingly. But what the August 2024 breach exposed is a widening gap between the physical security of infrastructure — the barriers, the cameras, the guards — and the digital security of the systems that control it. You can harden a Tube station against a physical attack. If two teenagers can walk through the digital front door, the hardening is decorative.

This gap is not unique to TfL. It exists in every institution that has digitised faster than it has secured. The NHS, local councils, energy providers — organisations that hold sensitive data on millions of people, running on systems that were not designed for a world in which eighteen-year-olds treat intrusion as a hobby. The TfL breach cost £39 million. The question nobody is asking publicly is: what is the next one going to cost, and who is already inside?

There is a particular kind of institutional failure that is harder to name than corruption or incompetence. It is the failure of imagination. The people who designed TfL's systems imagined threats: state actors, organised crime, sophisticated cyber-attacks with military-grade tools. They budgeted for those threats. They built defences against those threats. What they did not imagine was an eighteen-year-old in a council flat in Bow, livestreaming his intrusion on Telegram, treating their system like a level in a game he was playing for the entertainment of a group chat called Scattered Spider. The failure is not that the defences were weak. The failure is that the imagination of the institution could not conceive of the threat that actually arrived. And that failure of imagination is itself structural: institutions are designed to defend against the threats they can name. The threats that embarrass them — the bored teenager, the curious amateur, the inside-out breach that comes from a place of play rather than malice — those threats walk through the front door while the institution is staring at the horizon.
