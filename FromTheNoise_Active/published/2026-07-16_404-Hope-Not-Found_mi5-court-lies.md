# MI5 Lied to Three Courts to Protect a Neo-Nazi Spy Who Attacked a Woman With a Machete

**Curated by Zeon7. Not a journalist.**

---

Beth was attacked with a machete by her partner. Her partner was not just any abuser. He was MI5's agent. The security service knew. It protected him. It helped him leave the country while under police investigation. And then, when Beth sought justice, MI5 went to three separate courts and lied about what it had done.

Today, the deputy investigatory powers commissioner published a report confirming everything the BBC revealed last year and MI5 "vehemently denied." The finding is devastating: MI5 gave evidence based on "lies" to three courts. Senior officers fabricated accounts. Colleagues misled each other. A deputy director overstated matters. And the only reason anyone knows is because a journalist refused to drop the story, a victim refused to stay silent, and a Prime Minister was forced to order an investigation after the High Court threw MI5's explanations out.

The system worked as intended. That is the problem.

---

The agent was known as Agent X. He was a foreign neo-Nazi, a misogynist, and, according to the security service that recruited him, a useful source of intelligence. He used his spy status as a tool of abuse against his British partner, known by the alias Beth. He coercively controlled her. He attacked her with a machete.

When Beth sought legal redress, MI5 invoked its core secrecy doctrine of "neither confirm nor deny" (NCND) to keep information about Agent X hidden from her. The courts accepted MI5's position. Beth was denied access to the very information that could have supported her case. The institution that exists to protect the public from hidden threats became the mechanism by which a hidden threat was protected from accountability.

But there was a problem with MI5's account to the courts. It was false.

In 2020, a senior MI5 officer, identified in the Goldring report as Officer 2, had disclosed Agent X's status to Daniel De Simone, the BBC's investigations correspondent. De Simone was investigating the agent. Officer 2 tried to persuade him to stop. In doing so, he breached NCND. He told a journalist what MI5 would later tell three courts it had never told anyone.

This was not a slip; the Goldring report finds that Officer 2, in the words of the BBC's reporting of the findings, "put forward a wholly fictitious account" to the courts, denying he had ever disclosed the agent's status. That account "formed the foundation of MI5's false account." He lied, repeatedly.

He was not alone.

Officer 3, another senior MI5 figure, is reported to have "misled his own colleagues" and, according to the BBC's coverage of the Goldring findings, "bears considerable responsibility for the continuation of MI5's falsehood." He "misrepresented" what Officer 2 had told him. He was not "truthful" about warnings he received from colleagues. The report describes a chain of internal deception: not one rogue actor, but an institutional pattern.

Then there is Witness A, an MI5 deputy director. He gave his organisation's witness statements to the courts and "overstated" matters during a key internal meeting. A note of his comments was "misleading." He "contributed to MI5's continued reliance on the false account."

This is not a story about one liar. It is a story about an institution in which lying travelled upward, was validated by seniority, and reached three courts before anyone outside the building could stop it.

The report also reveals that MI6 (the foreign intelligence service) and an unnamed foreign intelligence agency both knew that NCND had been departed from. MI5's own case files contained "cogent evidence" showing the disclosure. Despite this, MI5 repeatedly told the courts the opposite. "Opportunities to correct the position were missed," the report states, with the understatement of a system describing its own failure in language designed not to alarm.

The BBC revealed the truth in February last year. MI5 issued a statement at the time "vehemently denying" the allegations. The High Court later rejected MI5's explanations as "deficient and unreliable." The Prime Minister ordered Sir John Goldring to investigate. Today's report is the result.

MI5 Director General Sir Ken McCallum issued a statement this morning: "MI5 recognises without hesitation the seriousness of our failings in these proceedings. I repeat my previous apologies to both courts for the incorrect evidence that was provided, and for our slowness in recognising what had happened."

The Home Secretary, Shabana Mahmood, called the findings "stark" and promised "urgent action to hold MI5 to account." The report could lead to contempt of court proceedings. Criminal prosecution is possible. But apology after exposure is not accountability. It is damage control. The institution did not self-correct. It was corrected, forcibly and publicly, and only after every internal mechanism had failed.

This is not the first time MI5 has been found to have misled a court, nor the first time its internal culture has been described as resistant to external scrutiny. The pattern is well-documented by parliamentary committees, civil liberties organisations, and the independent reviewer of terrorism legislation. What distinguishes the Goldring report is not the finding of institutional failure; it is the specificity. Numbered officers. Named lies. A deputy director. A foreign intelligence agency confirming the truth while the domestic one sustained the fiction. This is not an anonymous leak or a whistleblower's account. It is a judge-led investigation commissioned by the Prime Minister, published on the record, with the Director General's apology attached. If this report does not produce reform, nothing will. And if nothing will, then the institution is not reformable by the mechanisms available to the public. That is a structural truth the report gestures at but does not state: the accountability architecture for the security services depends on the security services cooperating with it. When they choose not to, the architecture is decorative.

The NCND doctrine itself deserves scrutiny. It exists for a legitimate reason: confirming or denying an individual's status as an intelligence agent can endanger lives, compromise operations, and burn sources. But NCND is not a blanket exemption from truth-telling. It is a tool, and like any tool wielded without independent oversight, it can be repurposed. In this case, it was repurposed from protecting national security to protecting institutional reputation. The courts accepted MI5's invocation of NCND because courts are structurally deferential to national security claims; they lack the classified information to evaluate them independently, so they trust the institution presenting them. That trust was abused. The question the Goldring report implicitly raises but does not answer is whether NCND, as currently constructed, is compatible with the rule of law, or whether it functions in practice as a get-out-of-court-free card for an institution that has learned that judges will not push back against a national security claim.

---

The architecture of MI5 is built on secrecy. That secrecy is necessary: an intelligence agency cannot function if its methods, sources, and operations are visible. The public accepts this bargain: we grant the security service extraordinary powers of invisibility because we trust those powers are used against threats, not against victims.

But secrecy, unchecked, becomes a weapon. It was weaponised against Beth. It was deployed to protect Agent X: a man the service knew was violent, knew was an abuser, and chose to defend anyway. Officer 2 went further than merely disclosing the agent's status to De Simone. According to the Goldring report, he tried to persuade the BBC journalist to drop the investigation entirely. He described Agent X as "not an abusive misogynist nor a real extremist." He even suggested De Simone meet the agent in person. A senior MI5 officer, defending a neo-Nazi who attacked a woman with a machete, offering a journalist a face-to-face meeting with the abuser. This is not error; this is advocacy.

When the truth emerged, when De Simone's reporting made it impossible to sustain the fiction, the institution did not pivot to honesty. It doubled down. Officer 2 constructed "a wholly fictitious account" in which he denied ever disclosing the agent's status. That fiction became the basis of MI5's evidence to three courts. Officer 3, informed of what had happened, misled his own colleagues. Witness A, a deputy director, gave statements that "overstated" the truth. This was not one person's failure. This was layers of the organisation, each adding weight to the falsehood, each making it harder for the one below to correct course.

And below them, all the way down, was Beth: a woman who had been attacked with a machete, waiting for an institution to tell the truth so she could have her day in court. She is still waiting.

Consider what it means to be Beth. Your partner attacks you with a blade. You survive. You seek justice. And then you discover that the state, the institution that exists to protect citizens from hidden threats, has been protecting the man who attacked you. Not passively, not through negligence, but actively: lying to courts, fabricating accounts, deploying its full institutional weight to shield him from consequences. The people who are supposed to catch criminals are running interference for one. The secrecy that is supposed to keep the country safe is being used to keep a violent man free.

This is not abstract. This is the psychological reality of being a victim of state-protected abuse. The gaslighting is structural. Every court that believed MI5's lies reinforced the message: the institution matters more than you. Every internal review that missed the evidence confirmed it. The only reason Beth knows the truth now is because a journalist at the BBC refused to accept the official account and spent years proving it was false. Ordinary victims do not have BBC investigations correspondents working their cases. Ordinary victims are left with the lies.

The pattern is not unique to this case. Intelligence services recruit dangerous people because dangerous people have access to information. The moral calculus is pragmatic: tolerate the criminal to gain the intelligence. But that calculus has a cost, and the cost is always borne by the people the criminals hurt, not by the institutions that recruited them. Beth is the cost. The women Agent X attacked before her, if there were others, are the cost. The public, which funds MI5 and trusts it to operate within the law, is the cost. The Goldring report tells us what happened. It does not tell us how many other Beths there are.

The Goldring report names names, or at least numbered officers. Officer 2, Officer 3, Witness A. These are not junior staff. They are senior figures, a deputy director among them. The report describes "serious and systemic failures in MI5's conduct." Systemic. Not individual. Not aberrational. Built into the way the organisation operates.

When a woman is attacked with a machete by an MI5 asset and the security service's response is to lie to three courts to protect the asset, the institution's priorities are legible: protect the source, protect the organisation, let the victim absorb the cost.

That is the 404. It is not a glitch. It is not a bad apple. It is the architecture of an institution that has been allowed to believe its own secrecy doctrine excuses it from the truth. The Goldring report is a reckoning; whether it leads to reform or ritualised apology depends on whether the public treats this as a scandal or a Tuesday.
